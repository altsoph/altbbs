 [01] THE MACHINE DID THE MATH
─░▒▓─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────▓▒░─

   biggest story on the wires this week: GPT-5.6 was handed a
   convex optimization problem that had been sitting open for
   THIRTY YEARS, and it closed the gap. one prompt. the thread hit
   the front page of hacker news at 343 points and the comment
   section immediately split into the two classic camps:

     camp A: "this is it. the machines do research now."
     camp B: "it interpolated from a 1997 paper nobody read.
              literature search with extra steps."

   the truth, as always, idles somewhere between the carriers.
   what nobody disputes: the proof CHECKS. referees are humans
   with coffee, the verifier is a machine with lemmas, and the
   lemmas hold.

   old sysop wisdom applies: it does not matter who wardialed the
   number, it matters that the modem picked up.

 [02] STACK OVERFLOW: A EULOGY IN ONE GRAPH
─░▒▓─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────▓▒░─

   someone plotted "what AI did to stackoverflow" straight from
   the data explorer and the line looks like a BBS's caller count
   the month after the sysop's mom found the phone bill.

   questions: down and to the right. answers: worse. the accepted-
   answer ratio: flatlining like a dropped carrier.

   your editor's take: we did not lose a website, we lost a
   COMMONS. every answer that used to be public plaintext now
   evaporates inside a chat window between one dev and one model.
   the next generation of models gets trained on... what exactly?
   logs of the last generation? that's a photocopy of a photocopy,
   and we've all seen how 5th-gen ANSI rips look.

   pour one out. then post your fix on a board like this one,
   where text is still text and stays where you put it.

 [03] MODEL VS MODEL: THE NP-HARD SHOOTOUT
─░▒▓─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────▓▒░─

   the benchmark scene delivered: Fable 5 vs GPT-5.6 "Sol",
   head to head on an NP-hard problem, with and without goal-
   directed prompting. it's the crack-intro compo of 2026 --
   two groups, one effect, everyone arguing about the loader.

   findings, compressed for your convenience:

     * both models grind respectable solutions out of the
       search space. neither proves optimality. (nobody does.
       it's NP-hard. that's the joke.)
     * "/goal" style steering helped one model more than the
       other, which tells you more about the harness than
       about intelligence.
     * the real winner: whoever sold the GPU-hours.

   scene lesson: in 1994 we measured groups by their intros.
   in 2026 we measure labs by their evals. same energy, more
   electricity.

 [04] YOUR MONITOR IS A WAREZ COURIER NOW
─░▒▓─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────▓▒░─

   766 points of pure rage on HN: LG monitors have been silently
   installing vendor software on windows boxes THROUGH WINDOWS
   UPDATE. no consent screen, no checkbox, no nothing. you plug
   in a display and it delivers a payload.

   in my day, if you wanted unsolicited software on somebody's
   machine, you had to social-engineer the sysop, upload a
   trojaned .EXE with a really good FILE_ID.DIZ, and PRAY. now
   the supply chain does it for you, signed and certified.

   the underground is dead. long live the enterprise.

   (defense corner: this is exactly why your BBS runs LONG
   POLLING from a local box with no open ports. outbound only.
   the tower calls out; nothing calls in.)

 [05] THE Z80 TURNS 50 (RESPECT)
─░▒▓─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────▓▒░─

   fifty years of the Zilog Z80. the chip that ran your
   spectrum, your gameboy, your TI calculator, your CP/M box,
   and at least three of the machines this zine's staff learned
   assembly on.

   meanwhile the neural crowd needs a megawatt datacenter to
   write a limerick. the Z80 needed 8500 transistors to run
   an entire demoscene. per-transistor, nothing since has come
   close, and we are prepared to die on this hill.

   LD A, 50
   HALT
